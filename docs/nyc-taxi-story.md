# Two Ride-Hailing Businesses Hidden in One City

At first, the NYC taxi data looked like a standard route-ranking exercise. A few pickup zones kept showing unusually high fares, unusually high tip behavior, or unusually wide gaps between what riders paid and what drivers received. But once we followed those signals across routes, zones, and operators, a much clearer picture emerged: this was not one market. It was two different ride-hailing businesses operating in the same city.

This was intentionally a structured analytics story, not a hybrid text-search story. The NYC taxi dataset is mostly numeric and categorical, so forcing a text_match query here would have been synthetic. For this data, the honest FerrisSearch showcase is fast-field filtering, grouping, and aggregation over a large distributed index.

The first business lives in pickup zone 132. This is the long-haul premium market. Trips from 132 are expensive, especially the flagship corridor 132 -> 265. For HV0005, that single route averaged a fare of 127.67, a driver payout of 83.49, and a platform spread of 44.18 per trip. Even HV0003, which ran far more volume on the same corridor, still showed that this lane is structurally rich: 6,531 trips and more than 201k in total platform spread. Zone 132 is where the biggest absolute dollars live.

The second business lives in pickup zone 138. This is not the longest-haul market, but it is the best-tipping premium pocket. Routes from 138 into destinations like 230, 161, 163, and 164 repeatedly posted tip rates around 11 percent to 12 percent. For HV0005, 138 -> 230 stood out with a 12.21 percent tip rate and an average platform spread of 31.73 per trip. In other words, 138 is where premium urban trips behave like premium trips, even when the raw fare is smaller than the airport-style lanes out of 132.

Once we compared operators, the story stopped being about routes and became about business models. Citywide, over the positive-fare slice of the dataset, HV0003 was the scale player: about 1.55 million trips, 25.22 average fare, 20.48 average driver pay, and an 81.22 percent driver share. HV0005 looked different. It ran far fewer trips, about 491k, but with a slightly higher average fare, a wider average platform spread (6.58 vs 4.73), a lower driver share (74.57 percent), and a higher tip rate (4.81 percent vs 3.74 percent). That is not a small route quirk. That is a different economic model.

The geographic breakdown explained how HV0005 pulls that off. Its advantage is not evenly distributed across the city. It is concentrated. Pickup zone 132 alone produced about 332k of total platform spread for HV0005, and 138 added another 252k. After that, the economics fall off sharply. The next-best HV0005 zone, 230, was only about 49k. That is a very different shape from HV0003, which showed meaningful spread across a much broader set of zones. HV0003 looks like the broad-coverage operator. HV0005 looks like a premium-pocket harvester.

The corridor-level head-to-head made the distinction even sharper. HV0003 often wins on absolute spread because it owns more volume. But HV0005 consistently wins on per-trip monetization inside the richest corridors. On 132 -> 265, HV0005 generated a much larger spread per trip than HV0003 (44.18 vs 30.87). On 138 -> 265, 138 -> 230, and the cluster of premium 138 routes, the same pattern held: lower volume, stronger margin per trip, and in many cases stronger tipping behavior. HV0003 wins the city by scale. HV0005 wins select corridors by yield.

That is what made this a good FerrisSearch story. We did not start with a dashboard or a prebuilt narrative. We started with one suspicious route, then used grouped SQL over fast fields to walk the problem upward: route anomaly, premium zone, operator split, citywide economics, zone concentration, then corridor concentration. On a modest local machine, most of the drill-down queries over 12 shards landed around 35ms to 60ms, and even the broader citywide grouped scans over about 2.04 million matching rows came back in roughly 126ms to 158ms. These were not polished benchmark-lab numbers. They were interactive query times on ordinary hardware, which is exactly why the story was easy to discover in the first place.

The final takeaway is simple. HV0003 is the scale operator. HV0005 is the premium-margin operator. One wins by being everywhere. The other wins by concentrating on the city's richest pickup zones and highest-yield corridors. FerrisSearch did not just rank trips. It exposed the strategy.
